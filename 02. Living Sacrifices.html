Living Sacrifices: Embracing the Journey of Wholehearted Devotion

Moses felt a gentle tug within him, drawing him towards the tabernacle where the air seemed to hum with the presence of God. "Moses," resonated the voice of the LORD, "speak to the children of Israel." With a quickened heart, Moses nodded, embracing this divine charge.

Approaching the assembled people, Moses relayed the LORD's message. "When you approach the LORD," he began, gesturing for emphasis, "bring offerings from your own flock or herd." His authoritative tone left no room for doubt. "Choose a male without blemish for the ascending sacrifice," he instructed, emphasizing the importance of willingness. "Present it willingly at the tabernacle entrance, before the dwelling of the LORD."

The people listened intently, murmuring agreement, mingling with the rustle of fabric and shuffling of feet. In this sacred dialogue between man and God, actions spoke louder than words, each step a testament to their devotion. This approach, this ascent, reflected their dedication to seeking the presence of the Almighty."

Within this narrative, the Hebrew terms "Vayikra," "qorban," and "olah" hold significant meaning. "Vayikra," meaning "And He called," symbolizes God's beckoning of Israel through Moses, a divine invitation to draw near. "Qorban," derived from "qarab," underscores the intimacy desired by God, emphasizing the closeness between the Creator and His people. Similarly, "olah," referring to a "burnt offering," symbolizes the offering's journey towards God's presence, a sacrificial act of devotion and surrender.

Together, these terms paint a picture of God's desired relational dynamics, highlighting not only His call but also the manner in which we are to approach Him. Implicit within this narrative is the requirement of willingness. For the offering to be acceptable, it must be given freely and willingly, reflecting the heart's devotion to the Almighty.

In essence, four principles emerge: being called by God, approaching Him with reverence, ascending to His throne through sacrificial devotion, and offering willingly and freely. This narrative echoes Paul's directive to "present your bodies as a living sacrifice" (Romans 12:1), inspiring us to live out these principles in our daily lives.

Offering Our Whole Selves to God: Paul's Call to Live Sacrificially

When Paul exhorted believers to "present your bodies as a living sacrifice" (Romans 12:1), he was tapping into deep symbolic meaning from the Old Testament sacrificial system. His words carried echoes of the Hebrew terms "qorban" (offering) and "olah" (burnt offering) - sacrifices where an animal was wholly consumed by fire, representing complete dedication to God.

For the ancient Israelites, these offerings embodied principles of humility, reverence, and trust as the worshiper approached the Lord. Every sacrifice was an act of contrition, obedience, and surrender of something precious to God. Paul cleverly employed this sacrificial imagery to instruct Christ's followers on the posture that should characterize our daily discipleship.

Just as those Old Testament offerings were unreserved and total commitments, Paul called believers to a unreserved obedience and devotion of self. To be a "living sacrifice" means holding nothing back from God - our hopes, ambitions, desires, skills, and circumstances are ALL yielded up for His purposes. It requires an ongoing, wholehearted submission and trust in our loving Creator. 

This sacrificial living is fundamentally an issue of the heart. Like the dynamics of ancient Hebrew worship, it flows from an awe, reverence, and adoration for God's holiness. We aren't begrudgingly offering up our lives, but are compelled by amazement at the boundless worth of knowing Him. Out of that overflow, we joyfully hold nothing back.

In essence, Paul was bridging the symbols of the old covenant with the realities of the new. The sacrificial system foreshadowed and prepared the way for a regenerated people completely devoted to God. Our lives, not the animals, become the fragrant offering to the Lord we worship and adore.  Whether at church, work, or home, we are called to walk in daily surrender - letting His Spirit shape us into "living" displays of God's greatness.

Thematically, this message of complete devotion and sacrifice is also conveyed in the following biblical texts:

  • In the Old Testament, we see foreshadows of this type of wholehearted worship. The Shema (Deuteronomy 6:4-9) called God's people to love the Lord with all their heart, soul, and strength - holding nothing back in their devotion. The prophet Hosea 6:6 declares God's desire for steadfast love, not mere outward sacrifices.

  • Likewise, the New Testament reinforces this posture. Jesus himself taught that the greatest commandments were to love God with one's entire being and to love one's neighbor (Matthew 22:37-39). The apostle Peter exhorted believers to be "living stones" being built up into a spiritual house for offering spiritual sacrifices (1 Peter 2:5).

  • From Genesis to Revelation, we see God's heart for His people to live with single-minded allegiance and sacrificial abandon to Him. Just as the Old Testament system prepared the way, now we are called to continually "present ourselves as living sacrifices" (Romans 12:1) - set apart, holy instruments for His glory.

 Our reasonable act of worship, therefore, is to hold nothing back, but freely yield our bodies, minds, and wills in service of the One who gave Himself fully for us. As we do, we become participators in God's grand story of redemption. (See also Psalm 51:17, Micah 6:8, Hebrews 12:28-29, 1 Peter 2:5, Philippians 2:3-4, and Ephesians 5:2)

Approaching God

At the core of this call to live as "living sacrifices" are foundational principles for how we are to approach and relate to God Himself. Humility, contrition, a desire to please Him, trust in His character, dependence on Him to meet our needs, and a continual searching and seeking for deeper relationship with Him - these are constants in our journey.

These elements encompass the spirit of true repentance, which is central to sacrificial living. Repentance involves acknowledging our wrongdoing with humility and genuine remorse. But it goes beyond regret - repentance means reorienting our lives towards sincerely desiring to align with the teachings and will of Yeshua the Messiah.

Repentance requires trusting in the character of God - believing in His mercy, grace, and readiness to forgive those who turn toward Him. It means recognizing our dependence on Yeshua's guidance, forgiveness and power to overcome our brokenness and live in righteousness. True repentance is actively seeking that deeper intimacy with our Lord through prayer, studying Scripture, and obeying His Word.

This posture of continual repentance is at the heart of the "living sacrifice." Just as the ancient offerings required inspection and preparation, so we must continually evaluate our lives, turning from sin and realigning our motives and actions to God's purposes. As we do, we are positioned to experience the ongoing renewal and transformation the Lord desires.

Approaching the Holy One requires this lifestyle of repentance - humbly turning from our self-centered ways and surrendering to God's Lordship in every area of life. It is the path of wholehearted obedience and trust that allows us to draw near and become fully devoted and spent for His glory.

In Conclusion

Repentance stands as the cornerstone of our relationship with God. It is a posture of humility and contrition before His holiness, coupled with an earnest desire to please and obey Him. Yet repentance is not merely a one-time act, but an ongoing journey of renewal - continually seeking the Lord, trusting in His loving character, and depending on Him to meet our needs. As we walk this path of repentance, we are ushered into deeper intimacy with our Heavenly Father. With open hands and contrite hearts, we become "living sacrifices," wholly devoted instruments through which He can work and display His resplendent glory. It is a surrendered life of joyful obedience, experienced as we lean into the principles exemplified in the sacrificial system - humility, trust, seeking, and complete yielding to the One who is endlessly worthy
