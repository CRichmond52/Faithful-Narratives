Revealing the Depth of Torah: A Journey to Godly Wisdom and Redemption

Often misconstrued as mere "law" in English translations, the word "Torah" holds a depth of meaning far beyond this narrow interpretation.

Centuries ago, Hebrew rabbis faced the daunting task of translating Scriptures into Greek, birthing the Septuagint. In the Septuagint, Torah was translated as "Nomos," a word that means more than legal statutes, a word that embraces a tapestry of traditions and cultural norms.

"Nomos," when not confined to a rigid legal code, embodies a living heritage'a source of nourishment, wisdom, and Godly ordinances; and in Hebrew, "Torah" encompasses more than a set of laws or commandments; it represents Godly guidance, instruction, and teachings that lead to a fulfilling life in accordance with the will of God.

Similarly, the Greek word "Nomos" does refer to law, but it carries a broader connotation, incorporating the idea of custom, tradition, and order. When translated from the Hebrew "Torah," "Nomos" should retain this multifaceted meaning, signifying not only legal prescriptions but also the cultural and spiritual heritage of the Hebrew language.

As a living heritage, the  word Greek "Nomos" embodies nourishment by providing sustenance for the soul through its teachings and principles. It offers wisdom by imparting insights into ethical behavior, moral conduct, and spiritual truths. Moreover, "Nomos" serves as a repository of Godly ordinances, guiding individuals in their relationship with God and their interactions with others.

In essence, the Greek word "Nomos," when understood in the context of the Hebrew "Torah," should represents a rich and dynamic source of spiritual nourishment, wisdom, and divine guidance that continues to shape the lives of believers and communities today.

Regrettably, subsequent translations like the Latin Vulgate and the King James Bible missed  that point and reduced Torah to mere "Lex" or "Law," stripping away its multifaceted richness.

Translating Torah as "Law" obscures its essence, confining its boundless significance within legalism's narrow confines. Consider Paul, deeply rooted in Rabbi Gamaliel's teachings and the Pharisaic tradition. For him, Torah wasn't just legal decrees; it was life's fabric, guiding every thought and action.

Paul's epistles, born from a lifetime of Scriptural study, reveal his grasp of Torah. He imparts not legalistic doctrine but a vibrant testament to God's revelatory transformative power.

In Paul's worldview, Torah wasn't static but dynamic'a living, breathing entity, an inexhaustible wellspring of wisdom. His encounter with Yeshua ignited a fervent desire to share the Gospel, not through legalism but radical transformation'an invitation to embrace God's liberating truth.

Paul's Gospel intertwines with his understanding of Torah'a sacred journey culminating in the realization of God's boundless grace. Let's reclaim Torah's essence'not as a rigid legal code but a divine roadmap, guiding us on a journey of discovery, transformation, and ultimate redemption.

Delving deeper, another principle from Proverbs 25:2 emerges: "It is God's glory to conceal a thing, but kings" glory to search out a matter." This verse underscores God's hidden truths within Scriptures.

This highlights two principles:

The Glory of God in Concealment:

  • This shows how great and majestic God is, with His endless wisdom and knowledge. When God hides things, it suggests there are truths and mysteries we can't fully grasp. It highlights that God's wisdom and purpose go beyond what we can understand.

  • This suggests humility as we stand before God, understanding that we can't know everything. Recognizing God's control in hiding things helps us to trust His wisdom and live humbly and respectfully.

The Glory of Kings in Discovery:

  • This shows how important it is for people to ask questions, explore, and find new things. It emphasizes how our intelligence, curiosity, and hard work help us discover truths, solve problems, and learn more.

  • This suggests that it's good to try to understand and gain wisdom because it helps us grow personally, makes society better, and allows us to fulfill our duties. Seeking knowledge and exploring mysteries are noble efforts that help us live. 

Typologically "kings" in Proverbs 25:2 represent believers, who are called kings and priests in Revelation 1:6, "And hath made us kings and priests unto God and his Father; to him be glory and dominion for ever and ever." (KJV)

Deuteronomy 29:29 emphasizes that while secret matters belong to God, believers should search His Word for those hidden treasures.

Pursing that admonition we find that the Hebrew word Torah is rich in symbolism and reveals a profound meaning. Torah in Hebrew is spelled Tav, Vav, Resh, Hey:

  • The Tav symbolically connects to the cross through its ancient form.

  • The Vav symbolically connects to nail through its ancient form.

  • The Resh symbolically connects to a man through its ancient form.

  • The Hey symbolically connects to a window (i.e. sight or vision) through its ancient form. 

Symbolically we can infer from the Hebrew word "Torah" a cross, a man, a nail, and vision or sight; forming the word picture of , "see the man nailed to the cross."

Another example is found in the four lettered name of God YHVH.

In Hebrew it is spelled Yod, Hey, Vav, Hey.

  • The Yod symbolically connects to hand.

  • The Hey symbolically connects to a window.

  • The Vav symbolically connects to nail.

  • The Hey symbolically connects to a window. 

Symbolically we can understand from the Hebrew word "YHVH" a hand, a window, a nail, and a window; forming the word picture of, "see the hand, see the nail."

The Hebrew word "Torah" stems from the Hebrew root "yara," and is spelled in Hebrew, Yod, Resh, Hey.

  • Yod is the picture of a hand that is shaped to work, thus means hand or work.

  • Resh means head or man.

  • Hey means to reveal, see, or what comes from.

Symbolically we can understand from the Hebrew word "Yara", "What the man must do to see."

Torah, therefore, is our avenue for gaining wisdom, insight, light, instruction, and direction.

"Torah" (the Word, the Truth, the Law) embodies Truth, Light, a Way to Life, Liberty, Freedom, and so much more.

Romans 7:12 tells us that Torah is holy, fair, and good. Ezekiel also supports this by saying that following Torah means following the Spirit. These are two clear principles that are difficult to ignore.

In this short compilation we have seen that Torah is much more than law. It encompasses light, life, freedom, truth, wisdom, and so much more. Torah, from my  perspective is as vast as God Himself.
